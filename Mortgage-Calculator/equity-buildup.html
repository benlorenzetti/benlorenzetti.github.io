Mortgage Preference

For fixed rate mortgages, the principal (P) remaining after the nth monthly payment is defined in terms of the previous month's principal, the monthly interest rate (i) and monthly payment (p).

Pn=i Pn-1 -p

The mortgage calculator is based a geometric series for this sequence over a mortgage term of (N) months to directly calculate the principal remaining in any given month. The result was:

Substituting n=0 into this equation demonstrates a surprising (unsurprising) result: the initial mortgage pricipal (P0) and monthly payment (p) are related linearly by an independent constant (RN) fixed by the monthly interest factor (i) and loan period (N).

The prior two articles used the linearity of P0p =RN to model a relationship between home prices and rent. However to fit this instantaneous home price model to real data, the differences from actual market prices were lumped into a previously unelaborated-upon scalar (c). This scalar suggests that Americans have a relatively consistent preference (i.e. pay more) for mortgages and their associated costs than for rent. To make use of this scalar (c) we need to account for the major difference between owning with a mortgage and renting—building up equity in the loan.

Nominal Equity Gain from a Loan

The nominal equity gain in the nth month of a mortgage is due to the difference in the balance owed between months n-1 and n and due to exposure to inflation (home appreciation) of the remaining loan liability (where π is monthly home appreciation factor).

Δ En-1,n =Pn-1 -Pn +(π-1) Pn-1
Δ En-1,n =Pn-1 -( iPn-1 -p ) +(π-1) Pn-1
Δ En-1,n =Pn-1 ( 1-i+π-1 ) +p

It also shows a second surprising (unsurprising) result—if real interest rates are zero, then nominal equity gains in the mortgage are equal to the monthly mortgage payment!
